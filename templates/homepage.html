LootMachinaX was the first 3D game I developed for a course called Game Design. They gave us the freedom to create a game within the theme "Ex-Lootbox". From there, I created a game where the goal was for the player to find different loot boxes scattered around map, while exploring a 3D environment. These boxes contained different augmentations (e.g. "super jump", "rush", "break crystals") for the main character, allowing him to reach areas of the map that were previously impossible.

This course and the development of this game made me realize that Game Development is one of my biggest passions in this area, since I often found myself happy to think I was going to work on this game.

The Island was a game created in the context of the Game Development Methodology course I took during my Master degree. Along side a small team of colleagues, we developed a 3D low poly style game that explored the basic concepts of a survival game such has harvesting materials, hunting animals, gathering resources and exploring.

The course was more focused on the whole game development experience, i.e., from early scratches and brainstorm to competition and domain analysis. Therefore the game itself focused basic survival game mechanics.

We had the privilege to work closely with two art students who developed specific assets for our game and helped us improve the overall look and feel of the game environment. Getting to work on this game with a team from distinct backgrounds was a great learning experience.

Way out is a 3D low poly puzzle game where you play as a golem who just woke up and is trying to figure out the purpose of his existence. To do so, he has to solve certain puzzles and challenges in different rooms and environments to find clues about himself and the reason of his embodiment.

The plot behind the game is that a human scientist managed to be the first in achieving full conscience transmutation. The puzzles that the golem has to solve throughout the game were actually created by the golem himself, while he still embodied his human form. They are a way of validating whether the golem’s cognitive and reasoning abilities are compatible with those of normal humans and if the transmutation occurred flawlessly.

Way Out was developed as a part of my Master thesis that aimed to study the players' attentional skills and mental workload in games.
